⚖️ Your Rights as a Child in Sri Lanka

Click any card to learn more. These are YOUR rights — the law protects them! 👇

🏫
Right to Education
Every child can go to school for FREE until age 16. No one can stop you.
Under Sri Lanka's Free Education Policy and Education Ordinance, every child has the right to free education. No school can turn you away because of your religion, language, ethnicity, or how much money your family has. Teachers are NOT allowed to physically punish you — caning is illegal. If your school treats you unfairly, a parent or guardian can complain to the Zonal Director of Education in your area.
Education Ordinance
🏥
Right to Health Care
You can get free medical treatment at any government hospital — always.
Sri Lanka gives free healthcare to every person, including all children. If you are sick, injured, or if someone hurts you, a hospital must treat you — even if you cannot pay. If someone physically hurts you, a doctor can write a medical report (called a medico-legal certificate) that can be used as evidence in court. You never need money to see a government doctor.
Public Health Act
🛡️
Right to Be Safe
No one is allowed to hit, hurt, threaten or abuse you — not even family members.
Sri Lanka's Penal Code and the Children and Young Persons Ordinance make it illegal for anyone to physically or emotionally abuse a child. This includes: hitting, burning, locking you up, refusing to feed you, or constantly saying things to make you feel worthless. The National Child Protection Authority (NCPA) exists specifically to protect children. If you are being abused, call 1929 — it is free, available 24 hours, and confidential.
NCPA Act 2000
💼
Right Against Child Labour
No one can force you to work instead of going to school. Children under 14 cannot be employed.
The Employment of Women, Young Persons and Children Act says that no child under 14 can be employed by anyone. Children aged 14–16 can only do light, safe work that does not hurt their health or stop them from going to school. If you are being forced to work instead of attending school, the Department of Labour can investigate and take action. You can also tell your school principal or a trusted teacher.
Labour Act
🗣️
Right to Be Heard
Your opinion matters! Adults must listen to your views in decisions that affect you.
Under the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC) — which Sri Lanka signed and agreed to follow — you have the right to express your views freely on any matter that affects you. In court cases involving children (like custody cases when parents separate), the judge must consider what the child wants. You also have the right to access information, and to freedom of thought and conscience.
UNCRC Article 12
📱
Right to Online Safety
Cyberbullying and sharing your photos without permission are serious crimes in Sri Lanka.
Sri Lanka's Computer Crimes Act and Penal Code protect you online. It is illegal to: send you threatening messages online, share your private photos without your permission, ask you for photos or personal information online, or follow/stalk you on social media. If something bad happens to you online, tell a trusted adult immediately. You can also report to the Computer Crime Investigation Division (CCID): 011 232 4444. Never share your phone number, address or school name online with people you don't know.
Computer Crimes Act
👨‍👩‍👧
Right to a Family
You have the right to know your parents and be cared for by your family.
Under the UNCRC and Sri Lanka's Children's Charter, every child has the right to know who their parents are and to be cared for by them when possible. If a child cannot live with their parents safely, the state has a responsibility to find a safe alternative — like a relative's care or a registered children's home. No child should be separated from their parents unless it is absolutely necessary for their safety. The Department of Probation and Child Care Services handles these situations.
Children's Charter
🏛️
Right to Legal Help
You have the right to free legal help if you need it — even if your family cannot afford a lawyer.
The Legal Aid Commission of Sri Lanka provides free legal assistance to people who cannot afford a lawyer — including children and their guardians. If a child is involved in a court case (as a victim, witness, or even as someone accused of something), they have the right to a lawyer. A child under 16 cannot be tried as an adult — they must appear in a special Juvenile Court. The Legal Aid Commission can be reached at 011 243 3618.
Legal Aid Commission Act

📚 Word Glossary — What Do These Words Mean?

Legal words can sound scary. Here they are explained simply.

NCPA
National Child Protection Authority — a government body in Sri Lanka that protects children from abuse. Their free helpline is 1929 (24 hours).
UNCRC
United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child — an international agreement signed by Sri Lanka that gives all children specific rights that governments must protect.
Penal Code
Sri Lanka's main book of criminal laws. It lists what actions are illegal and what punishment they carry. Hurting a child is a crime under the Penal Code.
Grama Niladhari
A government officer responsible for a village or small area. They can help families in need, connect people to social services, and report problems to higher authorities.
Legal Aid Commission
A government body that provides free lawyers to people who cannot afford one — including children and their guardians. Phone: 011 243 3618.
Juvenile Court
A special court for children under 16. It is different from an adult court — it focuses on the child's wellbeing and rehabilitation, not just punishment.
Medico-Legal Certificate
A document written by a doctor that records injuries or medical findings. It can be used as evidence in court if someone has hurt you.
